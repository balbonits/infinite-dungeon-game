# Design Pillars

These principles guide every design decision. If a feature conflicts with a pillar, the pillar wins.

## 1. Power Fantasy First

The player should feel like a growing god. No level cap. No stat ceiling. Early floors should become trivial as you level. Deep floors should make you sweat. The contrast IS the fantasy. Infinite depth with infinite progression means there is always something harder waiting below and always something easier to demolish above.

## 2. Dumb Controls, Smart Builds

Mash buttons to fight. Spend hours planning your build. The moment-to-moment gameplay is simple hack-and-slash -- a "dumb hack n slash" anyone can pick up in seconds. The metagame is deep theorycrafting: four stats with diminishing returns, three classes with distinct growth curves, hierarchical skill trees, and a six-affix equipment system. Simple to play, impossible to master.

## 3. The Dungeon Is Alive

The dungeon is not a container -- it is a character. It breeds monsters, adapts to players, and has its own goals. Floors are procedurally generated and grow larger and more complex as you descend. Every tenth floor, a boss blocks the exit. Challenge rooms tempt you off the main path. The deeper you go, the more the dungeon pushes back.

## 4. Deterministic Crafting, Exciting Drops

The Blacksmith gives you exactly what you ask for -- no RNG crafting. You pick the affix, you pay the materials, you get the result. The excitement comes from finding the right base items and rare materials in the dungeon. Control your build through crafting; be surprised by loot on the floor. Recycling at the Blacksmith ensures no drop ever feels wasted.

## 5. 15 Minutes to Satisfaction

A single play session -- one dungeon descent -- should feel complete in 15 to 30 minutes. Casual enough for a quick run, addicting enough to lose four hours chaining runs together. Death has consequences that scale with depth, but the town is always a safe reset point. No forced engagement. Respect the player's time.
